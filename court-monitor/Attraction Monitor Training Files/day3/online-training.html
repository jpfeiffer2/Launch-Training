Coverage may change

Your Assignment May Change

Some shifts may assign you to one attraction. Other shifts may require you to cover multiple attractions or a larger zone.

The standard stays the same

  • Stay active and keep scanning.
  • Move with purpose.
  • Correct unsafe behavior anywhere in your zone.

Guest engagement

Safety First, Fun Always

Strong Attraction Monitors do more than correct problems. When the area is under control, they help guests have fun without lowering the safety standard.

The rule that always comes first

  • Keep scanning before, during, and after guest interaction.
  • Engage only when you can still watch the area safely.
  • Fun never replaces the rules.

What good engagement sounds like

Safety + fun

Good examples

“How high can you jump?”
“Show me your best Superhero landing.”
“Nice job following the rules.”
“Great control — keep it up.”

Not okay

Encouraging flips or risky tricks.
Turning your back on the area.
Playing instead of monitoring.
Letting fun override the rules.
Watch firstKnow where guests are and what is happening.
Engage when safeEncourage safe play and create positive energy.
Stay within rulesKeep engagement safe, visible, and controlled.
The best monitors create safety and fun at the same time.
Stay alert on quiet shifts

Slow Days Are Not Easy Mode

A quiet floor still requires active monitoring. Fewer guests can make it easier to relax at the exact time you still need strong awareness.

Complacency is the risk

Standing still too long. Stopping your scan because the floor looks calm. Assuming fewer guests means fewer problems.

Why quiet shifts can still be difficult

Same standard
01

Fewer guests

Lower volume does not remove risk. Guests may spread across different areas, and one unsafe guest can still create a problem quickly.

02

Lower pressure

Quiet shifts make it easier to drift, get comfortable, talk too much, or stop moving with purpose.

03

Same standard

Scan, enforce rules, engage safely, keep the area guest ready, and call leadership when something is unsafe.

Slow does not mean safe by default.
Before opening

Daily Safety Checks

Daily Safety Checks are completed and logged before attractions open to guests.

Know the process

New monitors are not usually responsible for completing the official checklist on their own. This is normally handled by leadership or experienced team members.

Your role is to understand why the check matters and report anything unsafe you notice during your shift.

What the process is looking for

Logged inspection
01

Formal opening check

The checklist records who completed the inspection and when it was completed.

  • Employee / inspector
  • Date and time
  • Comments and verification
02

Attraction-specific items

Different parks and attractions have different equipment and safety concerns.

  • Pads, springs, straps, and netting
  • Airbags, blowers, alarms, and platforms
  • Electronics, props, lanes, and guest areas
03

Your responsibility

The opening checklist does not end your responsibility once the park opens.

  • Notice unsafe conditions
  • Report concerns immediately
  • Follow leadership direction
You may not complete the official checklist yet — but you are still responsible for helping keep the attraction safe.
Mid-shift issue

Problem Mid-Shift

If something becomes unsafe after opening, respond immediately. Do not wait or assume someone else already handled it.

What can change?

Even after an opening check, attraction conditions can change during the day.

Pads shift, netting tears, or equipment becomes damaged.
Trash, spills, or bodily fluids create a hazard.
Guests begin using an area in an unsafe way.

Use the response standard

Act immediately
1

Stop use

If the area is unsafe, stop play or use right away.

2

Protect area

Keep guests back and prevent the issue from getting worse.

3

Call leadership

Give the exact location and clearly explain what you found.

4

Wait for clearance

Support the response. Do not reopen the area on your own.

If something looks wrong during the shift, treat it like a live safety issue.
Call early and clearly

Communicate Clearly

When something needs leadership support, give information the team can act on quickly.

Do not be vague

  • “Someone needs help.”
  • “There is a problem.”
  • “Can a manager come here?”

Give the information leadership needs

Location + issue + need
1

Where?

Give the exact attraction or area.

2

What?

Briefly explain what is happening.

3

Need?

Say what support is needed.

Good examples

“Supervisor needed at Main Court. Pad has shifted.”
“Manager needed at Dodgeball. Guest refusing rules.”
“Bodily fluids near Launch N Dunk. Area is closed.”
“Code Blue at Stunt Bag. Leadership needed immediately.”
Clear communication gets the right help to the right place faster.
Repeat behavior

Repeat Rule Breakers

A guest who keeps ignoring the rules becomes a safety concern.

Do not get stuck in a loop

One reminder may be education. Repeated reminders mean the situation needs a firmer response.

  • Correct the rule clearly.
  • Watch for behavior change.
  • Escalate if it continues.

Change the response when behavior repeats

Safety concern
1

Correct clearly

State the rule and what needs to change.

2

Watch for change

Confirm the unsafe behavior actually stops.

3

Pause or remove

Follow park process if the guest keeps breaking rules.

4

Call leadership

Escalate repeated, defiant, or unsafe behavior.

Do not argueThe goal is not to win a debate with the guest.
Stay focusedThe goal is to keep the attraction safe.
Repeated rule breaking is not just annoying — it is unsafe.

Incident awareness

Details Matter

After something happens, leadership may need your observations to complete the report accurately.

NoGuessing
NoBlaming
NoExaggerating

Replay board

Stick to facts
1

Where?

Exact attraction, court, lane, or area.

2

When?

Approximate time or part of the shift.

3

What?

What you saw or heard, not guesses.

4

Who?

Guests, parents, team members, or leadership involved.

5

Your Action?

Stopped play, called leadership, cleared the area, or kept guests back.

Report what you saw, what you heard, and what you did. Do not fill in the blanks.
Emergency awareness

When Something Feels Serious

You are not expected to solve every emergency. You are expected to recognize it and call for help quickly.

Your role

Act early, keep the area controlled, and follow leadership direction.

  • Call leadership immediately.
  • Use Code Blue or Code Adam when needed.
  • Keep guests away from danger.
  • Do not handle it alone.

End of shift

Reset Your Area

Leave the attraction ready for the next monitor and the next group of guests.

Do not just walk away

Your replacement should not inherit a messy area, hidden issue, or unanswered safety concern.

  • Clean what you can.
  • Report what needs leadership.
  • Leave the area ready.

What Would You Do?

The right response usually starts with the same idea: protect guests first, then get the right help.

Think like a monitor

This is not about having the perfect wording. It is about choosing the safe next action.

  • Stop unsafe use.
  • Call leadership early.
  • Stay calm and controlled.
