Day 2 · Attraction awareness

Every Attraction Has a Risk Profile

Your job changes slightly depending on what can go wrong.

Narration Draft: Day 2 is about monitoring the core attractions safely. Every Launch park can look a little different, but there are several areas that most Attraction Monitors will see again and again: Main Court, Dodgeball, Ninja Course, and Launch Pad or Stunt Jump. Each attraction has its own risk profile. Main Court is about traffic and collisions. Dodgeball adds competition and fast decisions. Ninja Course involves obstacles and body control. Launch Pad adds height, timing, and landing safety. You do not need to memorize every attraction that may exist in every park. You do need to understand how to identify risk, enforce posted rules, and ask leadership when you are unsure.
DAY 2 · Slide 2
Main Court
Core attraction 1

Main Court: The Heart of the Park

Highest traffic means highest need for active supervision.

Narration Draft: Main Court is usually the busiest and most visible attraction in the park. It may have guests of different ages, sizes, and skill levels all using the area at the same time. That makes it one of the most important places for active monitoring. The biggest risks are collisions, double bouncing, guests sitting or resting on trampoline beds, running across the court, jumping too close to others, and guests failing to exit when their time is over. On Main Court, you should be constantly scanning across the whole area, not just watching the guest doing the biggest trick. Your goal is to keep movement controlled and spacing safe.
DAY 2 · Slide 3
Main Court
See it early

Main Court Risk Signals

Watch for the behavior that usually happens before the injury.

  • One guest affecting another guest’s bounce
  • Sitting, resting, or lying on trampoline beds
  • Running, roughhousing, crowding, or expired wristbands
Busy courts require constant scanning.
Narration Draft: On Main Court, many incidents are predictable if you know what to watch for. One guest affecting another guest’s bounce is a major concern, especially when there is a large size difference. Sitting or resting on a trampoline bed creates a risk that another guest may not see them. Running across the court, roughhousing, and crowding all increase collision risk. You should also watch for guests with expired wristbands or guests entering without the correct socks. If you see an unsafe pattern developing, act early. A quick correction now is better than clearing the court after someone is injured.

Scenario
Main Court challenge

One Court. Hundreds of Decisions.

A strong monitor prioritizes the next risk.

Scenario

Main Court is packed.

A small child is jumping near a larger teen, two guests are sitting, and a group is running across the court. What comes first?

Ignore it unless someone complains
Correct the highest immediate risk, then keep scanning and correct the rest
Focus only on the guests sitting down
Leave the court to find a manager
Narration Draft: A busy Main Court can present multiple problems at the same time. That is normal. Your job is to prioritize risk and keep working the area. If a small child is too close to a larger jumper, that may be the most immediate concern because a collision or bounce interference could happen quickly. Correct that first, then address the guests sitting and the group running. You should not freeze because there are several things happening at once. You also should not leave the court unless coverage is in place. Work the problems in order of urgency, use your whistle and voice, and call for support if the area is becoming too difficult to control alone.

Court Control
Authority to control the court

Pause Play When Needed

Stopping activity is sometimes the safest decision.

It is better to pause play early than explain later why you waited.

Narration Draft: Attraction Monitors have to be comfortable stopping activity when conditions become unsafe. That might mean clearing a section of Main Court, pausing a lane, stopping a Dodgeball game, or holding guests at the Launch Pad until the landing area is clear. Some new employees hesitate because they do not want to interrupt the fun. But pausing play is not a punishment. It is a safety tool. If guests are not listening, if equipment appears damaged, if an injury occurs, or if the court becomes too crowded to manage safely, stop the activity and call leadership. Guests may be briefly frustrated, but a controlled pause is far better than an avoidable injury.
DAY 2 · Slide 6
Dodgeball
Core attraction 2

Dodgeball Is Controlled Chaos

Competition increases energy, speed, and rule testing.

Narration Draft: Dodgeball can be one of the most exciting attractions in the park because guests are competing directly with each other. That excitement is also what creates risk. Guests may throw too hard, aim too high, ignore boundaries, argue about being out, rush the center line, or get too physical. Your job is not to remove the fun from the game. Your job is to keep the game controlled. That means explaining expectations, starting games clearly, watching both sides, using your whistle, and stepping in quickly when the game stops being safe. A great Dodgeball monitor brings energy, but also keeps the rules clear.
DAY 2 · Slide 7
Dodgeball
Enforce with confidence

Dodgeball Rules Need Energy

Use the whistle, use your voice, and keep the game moving.

  • Keep teams separated and boundaries clear
  • Call outs consistently
  • Stop rough or unsafe play immediately
Competition needs control.
Narration Draft: Dodgeball monitoring requires confidence. If you are quiet or uncertain, the game can get away from you quickly. Before play starts, make sure the guests understand the boundaries and basic expectations. During the game, keep your eyes moving between both teams. Use the whistle when someone is out, when play needs to stop, or when a behavior becomes unsafe. Be consistent. If you call one guest out for a rule, call the same rule the same way for the other side. Guests may disagree, but consistency protects both safety and fairness. If roughness, arguments, or repeated rule breaking continue, stop the game and call leadership if needed.

Scenario

Dodgeball Challenge

The game is only fun if it stays controlled.

Scenario

A guest keeps arguing every out.

They are getting louder, other players are reacting, and the game is losing control. What should you do?

Let the teams work it out
Argue back until they agree
Pause the game, address the behavior calmly, and call management if it continues
End every Dodgeball game as soon as someone complains
Narration Draft: Dodgeball arguments can escalate quickly because guests are already competitive. If one guest keeps arguing every call, the issue is no longer just one out. It is the control of the attraction. Pause the game if needed, address the behavior calmly, and reset the expectation. You might say, “We need to keep the game moving and safe. I will make the calls, and arguing every play cannot continue.” If the guest continues, call a Supervisor or Manager. Do not argue back, and do not let the game turn into a shouting match. Your tone should stay professional, but your control of the court should be clear.
DAY 2 · Slide 9
Ninja
Core attraction 3

Ninja Course Basics

Guests are testing balance, grip, and body control.

Narration Draft: The Ninja Course is different from a trampoline court because guests are moving through obstacles that challenge balance, grip strength, and body control. The risks often come from guests entering before the path is clear, climbing or using obstacles incorrectly, carrying loose items, or attempting movements beyond their ability. Your job is to control flow and watch body position. Guests should not crowd each other on obstacles, cut across the course, or use elements in a way they were not designed to be used. Ninja Course monitoring is about patience and spacing. The safest course is one where each participant has enough room and understands how to move through the obstacles properly.
DAY 2 · Slide 10
Ninja
What to watch

Ninja Course Risk Signals

Loose items, crowding, and poor control create preventable incidents.

  • Empty pockets and remove loose items when required
  • Keep guests from crowding obstacles
  • Stop unsafe climbing, hanging, or shortcutting
Control flow through each obstacle.
Narration Draft: On the Ninja Course, watch for loose items like phones, jewelry, watches, keys, or anything that could fall, strike another guest, or get damaged. Watch for crowding at the start of obstacles and guests entering before the person ahead has cleared the area. Also watch for body control. If a guest is swinging wildly, climbing outside the intended path, hanging on equipment too long, or shortcutting through the course, correct them early. Many Ninja Course issues happen because guests are excited to try again quickly. Your role is to slow the pace enough to keep the course controlled and safe.

Ninja Course Challenge

Flow control prevents collisions and falls.

Scenario

Three guests rush onto the same obstacle.

The first guest is struggling, and the next two are already climbing behind them. What do you do?

Let them continue because they chose to go
Tell the back two guests to wait until the obstacle is clear
Only correct them if someone falls
Leave to find a manager before saying anything
Narration Draft: This is exactly the type of moment where an Attraction Monitor needs to act early. If one guest is struggling and two more guests are entering the same obstacle behind them, the risk is crowding, contact, and panic. Stop the back two guests and have them wait until the obstacle is clear. You can keep the tone simple: “Hold up for me. Let them clear the obstacle first.” You are not criticizing their ability. You are controlling spacing. If guests continue to ignore spacing or use the obstacles incorrectly, call leadership. The Ninja Course should feel exciting, but it should never feel like guests are piling onto each other.
DAY 2 · Slide 12
Launch Pad
Core attraction 4

Launch Pad / Stunt Jump

Height plus timing means the monitor must control every jump.

Narration Draft: Launch Pad, sometimes called Stunt Jump, is one of the clearest examples of why Attraction Monitors cannot be passive. Guests are jumping or dropping from a platform into a landing area, which means timing matters. The next participant should not enter or jump until the previous participant has fully cleared the landing area. Guests may also attempt unsafe landings, hesitate at the edge, jump before instructed, or try to perform tricks they cannot control. Your job is to control the sequence: wait, instruct, jump, clear, reset. This attraction should never feel like a free-for-all. One participant at a time and a clear landing area are the foundation.
DAY 2 · Slide 13
Launch Pad
Sequence matters

Launch Pad Rules

Nobody jumps until the landing area is ready.

1Wait for instruction
2Jump only when clear
3Exit landing area quickly
Narration Draft: The safest Launch Pad operation follows a simple rhythm. First, the participant waits for the monitor’s instruction. Second, the monitor confirms the landing area is clear. Third, the participant jumps or drops in the approved manner. Fourth, the participant exits the landing area quickly so the next guest can go. Problems happen when guests rush that sequence. Someone jumps too soon, another guest stays in the landing area, or a participant tries a landing that is not allowed. Your instructions should be clear and direct. If a guest is not listening, pause the attraction and reset expectations before allowing the next jump.

Scenario
Decision slide

Launch Pad Challenge

Control the platform before the jump happens.

Scenario

A guest starts counting down for themselves.

The landing area is not clear yet, but they bend their knees and look ready to jump. What do you do?

Let them jump because they already started
Immediately stop them and hold the jump until the area is clear
Look away and hope they wait
Tell the person in the landing area to move faster but allow the jump
Narration Draft: This is a high-risk moment because the unsafe action has not happened yet, but it is about to. Stop the guest immediately and hold the jump until the landing area is clear. Use a strong, clear instruction: “Stop. Wait for me.” Do not rely on the guest to make the right decision once they are already preparing to jump. Launch Pad requires monitor-controlled timing. If a guest repeatedly tries to jump before being instructed, remove them from the sequence and call leadership if needed. It is much easier to stop a jump before it happens than to respond after someone lands on another guest.

Awareness
Awareness only

Other Attractions at Launch

Different parks have different attractions. Do not guess.

  • Read and enforce posted rules
  • Follow local training and manufacturer requirements
  • Ask a Supervisor when unsure
Local attractions require local training.
Narration Draft: Many Launch locations have additional attractions such as laser tag, XP Arena, climbing walls, ropes, arcade areas, bowling, VR, Lucky Putt, or other features. This training is not trying to make you an expert on every possible attraction in every park. Instead, the standard is this: follow the posted rules, follow the training provided at your location, and never guess if you are unsure. If an attraction looks unsafe, if guests are using it incorrectly, or if you do not know the correct procedure, stop and ask a Supervisor. Guessing can create risk. Asking for help protects you, the guest, and the park.
DAY 2 · Slide 16
Inspection
Look beyond guest behavior

Spotting Unsafe Conditions

Equipment problems must be reported before guests get hurt.

  • Loose, damaged, or worn padding
  • Rips, tears, exposed metal, or unsecured netting
  • Debris, spills, biohazards, or blocked areas
Unsafe equipment changes the decision.
Narration Draft: Attraction Monitors are not only watching guests. You are also watching the attraction itself. Look for loose padding, damaged pads, ripped trampoline beds, exposed metal, unsecured netting, debris, spills, or anything that looks out of place. If something appears unsafe, do not assume it is probably fine. Clear the area if needed and call management. Some issues can be corrected quickly. Others require the attraction or section to remain closed until repaired. The key is that equipment concerns must be communicated before guests continue using the area. A guest should never be the first person to discover a hazard by getting hurt on it.
DAY 2 · Slide 17
Checks
Checklist mindset

Daily Safety Checks

Opening checks are not paperwork. They decide whether an attraction is ready.

  • Complete before opening attractions
  • Document deficiencies clearly
  • Verbally report serious concerns to management
Safety checks protect the day before it starts.
Narration Draft: Daily safety checks are not just a form to complete because someone told you to. They are the process that confirms an attraction is ready before guests use it. Checks may include pads, beds, springs, D-rings, redundant beds, netting, cleanliness, and any attraction-specific items at your location. If something is missing, damaged, loose, or questionable, document it and communicate it to the Manager on Duty before opening the area. Do not assume the next person will notice. Do not assume a problem is minor because the park opened that way yesterday. Every day starts fresh, and every day the attraction must be safe before guests use it.

Day 2 Floor Challenge

Your trainer will validate attraction control, not just attraction knowledge.

  • Demonstrate Main Attraction monitoring
  • Demonstrate Dodgeball, Ninja, and Launch Pad control
  • Identify unsafe conditions and explain next steps
Practice the decisions before working alone.
Narration Draft: Day 2 focused on the core attractions and the decisions that come with each one. Now your trainer should watch you apply it on the floor. You should practice scanning and correcting on Main Court, controlling a Dodgeball game, managing spacing on Ninja Course, and controlling the jump sequence on Launch Pad or Stunt Jump if your location has it. You should also practice identifying unsafe equipment or conditions and explaining what you would do next. The goal is not to know every attraction in the company. The goal is to show that you can think like an Attraction Monitor: see the risk, control the activity, communicate clearly, and ask for support when needed.
